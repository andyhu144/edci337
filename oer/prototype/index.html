OER: Design Principles for Non-Designers

An open educational resource teaching cognitive load theory, visual hierarchy, and accessible design to people who build presentations, documents, and digital content but have no formal design training.

Andy HuMarch 2026OER Prototype · 5%
Understand

The Challenge

Most people who create educational content, whether slides, handouts, reports, or web pages, have never studied design. They make decisions about layout, color, typography, and information density based on instinct or by copying whatever template they inherited. The result is content that often violates basic principles of how human cognition processes visual information: too much text on a single slide, labels separated from the diagrams they describe, decorative images that add cognitive load without supporting the learning objective.

The challenge is not that these people lack design talent. It is that they lack a framework for making design decisions deliberately. Mayer's multimedia learning principles, cognitive load theory, and UDL guidelines provide exactly that framework, but they are typically taught in education or psychology courses, not in the workplaces and classrooms where the design decisions are actually being made.

Audience: Working professionals, educators, and students who regularly create presentations, documents, or digital content but have no formal design training. Ages 20 and up. Comfortable with basic digital tools (PowerPoint, Google Slides, Canva) but not with design software or terminology.

Point of View Statement: Non-designers who create educational and professional content need a practical, theory-grounded framework for making visual design decisions because their current approach relies on instinct and templates that often work against how people actually process information.

Learning Objectives

What Learners Will Be Able to Do

  • Identify and name the cognitive load principle being violated in a given design example
  • Apply the contiguity, signaling, and coherence principles to improve an existing slide or document
  • Evaluate a piece of visual content using a simple rubric grounded in Mayer's principles
  • Choose appropriate text and image combinations that support dual coding rather than creating redundancy
  • Make one accessibility improvement (contrast, alt text, heading structure) to any piece of content they create

Plan

User Journey & Storyboard

The OER is structured as a single-page interactive website with four learning modules. The user progresses from understanding what cognitive load is (pretraining), through three practical application modules (contiguity, coherence, accessibility), each featuring a live interactive demo followed by a self-assessment exercise. This follows Merrill's sequence: problem context, activation of prior knowledge through relatable examples, demonstration through interactive visuals, application through hands-on exercises, and integration through a final self-audit tool.

The non-linear structure allows the user to jump to any module via navigation, supporting UDL's principle of multiple means of engagement by letting learners control their own pace and sequence. Each module is self-contained so it can be completed independently or as part of the full sequence.

Theory Connections

How the Design Applies Course Principles

1
Cognitive Load (Sweller / Mayer) Every interactive demo is designed to make the user experience cognitive load reduction firsthand. The slider in Module 1 lets them feel the difference between a cluttered and clean layout rather than just reading about it. This applies the modality principle: experiencing the concept through interaction rather than text alone.
2
Active Learning (ICAP / Merrill) Each module moves the learner from passive (reading about a principle) to constructive (manipulating a live demo) to interactive (applying the principle to their own work in the self-audit). This maps directly to the ICAP framework and follows Merrill's problem, demonstration, application sequence.
3
Universal Design for Learning Multiple means of representation: each concept is presented as text, as an interactive visual, and as a hands-on exercise. Multiple means of engagement: users control their pace and can jump between modules. Multiple means of expression: the self-audit can be completed mentally, on paper, or digitally.
4
Accessibility by Design Module 4 teaches accessibility, but the entire OER practices it. High contrast, semantic headings, keyboard navigation, descriptive labels on all interactive elements. The medium demonstrates the message: the website itself is an example of the design principles it teaches.
