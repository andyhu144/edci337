Cognitive Load

Your Audience Has Limited Brain Power

Working memory can only hold a few things at once. Every design choice either uses that capacity wisely or wastes it (Sweller, 1988).

Mayer's Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning identifies three types of cognitive load: extraneous (caused by bad design), intrinsic (caused by the complexity of the content), and germane (the useful mental effort of actually learning). Your job as a designer is to minimize the first so the learner has capacity for the third.

The Water Cycle: How Precipitation Forms in Earth's Atmosphere

Water evaporates from oceans, lakes, and rivers due to solar energy. As warm, moist air rises, it cools and condenses around tiny particles in the atmosphere, forming clouds. When water droplets in clouds combine and become heavy enough, they fall as precipitation (rain, snow, sleet, or hail).

Fun fact: A single cloud can weigh more than a million pounds! This might seem surprising, but the water droplets are spread over a huge volume of air.

Contiguity Principle

Keep Related Things Close Together

People learn better when corresponding words and pictures are near each other rather than far apart (Mayer, 2022).

The contiguity principle is one of the most commonly violated design rules. Labels in a separate legend, captions below a fold, explanations on a different slide from the diagram they reference. Every time the learner has to mentally connect separated elements, they spend cognitive resources on integration rather than learning.

Coherence Principle

If It Does Not Teach, Cut It

People learn better when extraneous material is excluded rather than included (Mayer, 2022). Every decorative element consumes cognitive resources.

Accessible Design

Design for Everyone

Disability is a mismatch between a person's abilities and their environment (Holmes, 2020). Fixing the environment fixes the problem.

Accessibility features benefit all users, not just those with disabilities. Captions help in noisy rooms, high contrast helps in sunlight, clear headings help everyone scan content faster. The UDL framework (CAST, 2018) formalizes this as providing multiple means of representation, engagement, and expression.
