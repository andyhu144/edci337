EDCI 337 — Multimedia Challenge: Comics

The Greenhouse Effect

Understanding Climate Change, One Panel at a Time
Andy Hu  |  February 21, 2026  |  University of Victoria

I'm creating an educational comic about the greenhouse effect and how it contributes to climate change. I chose this topic because climate change is one of the most important issues facing our generation, yet many university students struggle to explain the basic science behind it. Terms like "greenhouse gases," "infrared radiation," and "carbon emissions" come up constantly in the news and in campus conversations, but I've noticed that most people—myself included, before researching this project—couldn't clearly walk through the actual mechanism of how the greenhouse effect works.

My goal is to break down this invisible atmospheric process into a simple, visual, step-by-step explanation that anyone can understand in a few minutes. The comic format works perfectly here because the greenhouse effect is inherently sequential: sunlight comes in, gets absorbed, heat gets emitted, gases trap it, the planet warms. Each step maps naturally onto a panel, making the science feel like a story rather than a lecture.

The Process

Phase 1: Understand (Discover, Interpret, Specify)

Describe the Challenge

Climate change is widely discussed but poorly understood at a mechanistic level. University students frequently encounter terms like "greenhouse effect" and "carbon emissions" in news media, social media, and campus conversations, yet many cannot explain the step-by-step process of how greenhouse gases actually trap heat in the atmosphere. This gap between awareness and understanding limits students' ability to engage meaningfully with environmental discussions, evaluate proposed solutions, or make informed decisions about sustainability in their daily lives.

Context and Audience

The primary audience for this comic is university students, typically between the ages of 18 and 25, who are taking introductory environmental science, geography, or general education courses, or who simply want to understand the science behind climate change. These students are digitally literate, accustomed to consuming information through visual media, and tend to prefer concise, engaging content over dense textbook explanations. Many of them hear about climate change daily in the news and on social media but feel unable to explain the basic mechanism behind it when asked. This creates a frustrating gap: they care about the issue but lack the scientific vocabulary and conceptual understanding to discuss it with confidence.

From a psychographic perspective, these students value efficiency in their learning. They want to understand concepts quickly without wading through overly technical language. Many are motivated by a genuine concern for the environment but find that their understanding is limited to surface-level talking points—"carbon is bad," "the planet is warming"—rather than actual scientific literacy about why these things happen. They are juggling multiple courses, part-time jobs, and social commitments, so learning materials that are visually engaging and can be consumed in a short sitting are ideal. They are also likely to share well-designed educational content with peers on social media or in group chats, which means an effective comic could reach well beyond the immediate reader.

A typical case might be a second-year university student who has heard the term "greenhouse effect" dozens of times and knows it has something to do with carbon dioxide and warming, but cannot explain the step-by-step mechanism of how sunlight enters the atmosphere, gets converted to heat, and gets trapped by greenhouse gases. They may confuse the greenhouse effect with the ozone layer, or think that the greenhouse effect itself is inherently bad rather than understanding that it is a natural process that has been amplified by human activity. This student would benefit from a visual, sequential explanation that builds understanding one step at a time, connecting each part of the process before introducing the next.

An extreme case could be a student who is deeply anxious about climate change but feels powerless because they don't understand the science well enough to have informed conversations or evaluate proposed solutions critically. Without mechanistic understanding, they may accept or reject claims about climate change based on emotion or social pressure rather than scientific reasoning. On the other end, a student who is skeptical about climate change might benefit from seeing the straightforward physics of how greenhouse gases trap heat, presented without political framing—just the science. Additionally, students with varying levels of science background must be considered. Some may have strong physics or chemistry foundations while others may have none, so the comic must be accessible without being condescending. Accessibility considerations include ensuring sufficient contrast between text and backgrounds (the black-and-white manga style naturally provides high contrast), using clear and readable fonts, structuring content in a logical sequential flow, and providing descriptive alt-text for readers using screen readers.

POV Statement

A university student with limited understanding of atmospheric science needs a clear, visual, step-by-step explanation of how the greenhouse effect works so that they can understand the mechanism behind climate change, engage meaningfully in environmental discussions, and make informed decisions about sustainability.

Learning Objectives

Main Learning Objective

After reading this comic, students will be able to describe the step-by-step process of the greenhouse effect, from incoming solar radiation to heat being trapped by greenhouse gases, and explain how this process connects to climate change.

Sub-Learning Objectives

  • Students will be able to explain the difference between incoming solar radiation and outgoing infrared radiation in the context of Earth's energy balance.
  • Students will be able to identify the major greenhouse gases (carbon dioxide, methane, water vapor) and describe their role in trapping heat in the atmosphere.
  • Students will be able to explain why the greenhouse effect, while natural and necessary for life on Earth, becomes problematic when amplified by increased greenhouse gas concentrations from human activity.
  • Students will be able to connect the greenhouse effect to climate change and articulate why reducing greenhouse gas emissions matters for the planet's future.

Secret Learning Objectives

  • Build a sense of scientific agency by helping students feel literate enough to engage in environmental conversations and decision-making, rather than feeling excluded from the discussion.
  • Develop scientific literacy by demonstrating that complex atmospheric processes can be understood through accessible visual media—science is not reserved for experts.
  • Encourage metacognitive reflection on how step-by-step visual explanations can make abstract processes easier to learn and retain, fostering an appreciation for multimedia as a learning tool.

Phase 2: Plan (Ideate, Sketch, Elaborate)

Ideation

I brainstormed several approaches for teaching the greenhouse effect through a comic, evaluating each against the learning objectives and the multimedia design principles we've covered in EDCI 337:

Idea 1: Infographic-style panels. Each panel would be a labeled diagram showing one step of the greenhouse effect, similar to a textbook figure but arranged in comic panel format. This would be informative but potentially dry and passive—more like a textbook with illustrations than a true comic. Research on narrative and retention suggests that storytelling activates multiple brain regions and enhances information recall compared to bare factual presentation (Glonek & King, 2014). Without a narrative thread, readers might lose interest before finishing all 12 panels. This approach would also miss the opportunity to leverage the personalization principle (Mayer), since infographic-style labels tend toward clinical, formal language.

Idea 2: Anthropomorphized gas molecules as characters. CO2, methane, and water vapor molecules would be characters with distinct personalities, and the story would follow them as they trap heat. This approach has strong storytelling potential and would create emotional engagement through character development. However, it risks overcomplicating the science by attributing motivations and actions to molecules, potentially creating misconceptions. It could also increase extraneous cognitive load (Sweller) by forcing readers to track multiple characters, their personalities, and their relationships while simultaneously learning the atmospheric process. The split attention between character narrative and scientific content could undermine learning rather than support it.

Idea 3: A narrator-guided walkthrough with a consistent character. A manga-style character guides the reader through the greenhouse effect step by step, with each panel showing one part of the process using clear visuals and simple captions. The character asks questions the reader might have ("What are greenhouse gases?") and provides answers, creating a conversational, approachable tone. This approach balances engagement with clarity: the character provides narrative continuity and emotional connection without overcomplicating the science. It naturally applies the personalization principle (conversational tone), the segmenting principle (one step per panel), and the contiguity principle (labels placed directly on diagrams).

Idea 4: A time-travel narrative. A student travels back in time to see Earth without greenhouse gases (frozen), then to the present (comfortable), then to the future (overheated). While dramatic and emotionally compelling, this approach splits attention between the time-travel plot and the actual science, increasing extraneous cognitive load. It also requires too many panels for narrative setup (establishing the time-travel concept, introducing the character's motivation), leaving fewer panels for the actual scientific explanation. Given the 12-panel constraint, this approach would sacrifice depth of explanation for narrative complexity.

I selected Idea 3 as my final direction because it best satisfies the learning objectives while keeping the comic simple, clear, and engaging. The consistent narrator character provides a storytelling thread that maintains reader interest (Glonek & King, 2014) without introducing unnecessary complexity. The step-by-step panel structure maps directly onto the sequential nature of the greenhouse effect, and the conversational tone supports the personalization principle (Mayer). This approach also keeps each panel focused on a single concept, managing cognitive load through segmenting (Sweller). Comics research supports this choice: Tribull (2017) argues that sequential visual narrative is particularly effective for communicating scientific processes because it mirrors the step-by-step nature of scientific reasoning, and Morrison, Bryan, and Chilcoat (2002) found that student-generated comics enhance engagement and comprehension by giving learners ownership over the visual representation of concepts.

Storyboard / Script

Below is the panel-by-panel script that guided the comic's creation.

Panel 1 — Introduction
Scene: A girl with short hair shown from the waist up, facing the viewer with a cheerful expression. Simple background.
Text: "Let's learn about the greenhouse effect!"
Panel 2 — Sunlight Arrives
Scene: View from space looking down at Earth. The sun in the upper right with rays streaming down toward the planet's curved surface. Stars and dark space above, thin atmosphere visible along the horizon.
Text: "The Sun shines down on Earth."
Panel 3 — Reflection and Absorption
Scene: Earth's surface and atmosphere from space. Arrows pointing down as incoming sunlight, some arrows bouncing back upward off the surface into space showing reflection. Cloud layers along the curved horizon, stars above.
Text: "Some of the sunlight is reflected back into space." Label: "Absorption"
Panel 4 — Infrared Radiation
Scene: Earth's curved surface across the middle of the frame. Wavy zigzag arrows radiating upward from the surface into space, representing heat energy.
Text: "The Earth then emits heat as infrared radiation." Label: "Infrared Radiation"
Panel 5 — Greenhouse Gases Trap Heat
Scene: Cross-section of Earth's atmosphere. Surface at the bottom with wavy infrared arrows rising upward. A hazy band in the atmosphere representing greenhouse gases deflects some arrows back downward while a few pass through into space.
Text: "Greenhouse gases in the atmosphere trap some of this heat."
Panel 6 — Heat Trapped (Visual)
Scene: Atmospheric diagram showing Earth's surface at the bottom and a cloudy gas layer above. Wavy heat arrows bouncing back and forth between the ground and the gas layer, unable to escape.
Label: "Heat Trapped"
Panel 7 — Question Panel
Scene: The girl from Panel 1 shown from the shoulders up with a curious expression, head tilted slightly, looking directly at the viewer. Simple background.
Text: "What are greenhouse gases?"
Panel 8 — Naming the Gases
Scene: Three spherical molecular models floating against a cloudy atmospheric background. Each molecule is a distinct cluster of shaded spheres representing the three major greenhouse gases.
Text: "They include carbon dioxide, methane, and water vapor."
Panel 9 — Molecular Diagrams
Scene: Three molecular diagrams side by side against a cloudy background. CO2 as three spheres in a line, H2O as three spheres in a bent V-shape, CH4 as one larger sphere surrounded by four smaller spheres.
Text: "CO2" / "H2O" / "CH4" (labels on diagrams)
Panel 10 — Heat Stays In
Scene: Atmospheric cross-section with Earth's surface along the bottom and a cloudy gas layer above. Wavy arrows rise from the surface and curve back downward off the gas layer, looping between the ground and atmosphere.
Text: "These gases keep heat in the atmosphere." Label: "Heat Stays In"
Panel 11 — Planet Warming
Scene: Full view of Earth from space, centered. Wavy heat lines and a glow radiating outward from the planet's surface.
Text: "This warms the planet!" Label: "Warming Up!"
Panel 12 — Consequences and Call to Action
Scene: Wider panel. Two characters standing side by side facing the viewer — the girl narrator from earlier and a boy with short spiky hair. Behind them a landscape of melting icecaps and rising water.
Text: "Too much warming leads to climate change... So, we need to protect our planet!"

Theory Applied

This comic is guided by several multimedia learning theories and principles covered in EDCI 337. Below, I explain each principle, how it is applied in the comic's design, and why it supports the learning objectives.

Mayer's Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning & Dual Coding

The comic applies dual coding theory (Paivio) by pairing visual illustrations with concise text in every panel, allowing readers to process the greenhouse effect through both the visual and verbal channels simultaneously. For example, Panel 4 shows wavy lines radiating upward from the Earth (visual channel) while the caption explains "The Earth then emits heat as infrared radiation" (verbal channel). By encoding the same concept through two separate neural pathways, the reader builds a stronger, more retrievable mental representation of the process (Schnotz, 2022). This is the core of Mayer's multimedia principle: people learn more effectively from words and pictures together than from words alone. Each step of the greenhouse effect is both shown and described, ensuring that readers who are stronger in either channel can still access the content.

Cognitive Load Theory: Managing Extraneous Load

Cognitive load theory (Sweller) informs several key design decisions aimed at reducing extraneous cognitive load—the mental effort wasted on poor design rather than actual learning. The coherence principle is applied by using a clean black-and-white manga art style with no unnecessary decorative elements. Every visual element in each panel serves the learning objective; there are no background details or artistic flourishes that would distract from the science. The contiguity principle is applied by placing text labels directly on or adjacent to the relevant visual element. For instance, the label "Infrared Radiation" appears right next to the heat waves in Panel 4, and "Heat Trapped" is placed directly over the trapping diagram in Panel 6. This spatial proximity eliminates the need for readers to split their attention between separated text and images, reducing the cognitive effort required to make connections. The signaling principle is used through bold labels like "Absorption," "Infrared Radiation," "CO2," "H2O," "CH4," and "Heat Stays In" that direct the reader's attention to the most important scientific concepts in each panel. These labels function as visual cues that highlight key information, preventing readers from wasting cognitive effort determining what is important.

Cognitive Load Theory: Managing Intrinsic Load

Intrinsic cognitive load is managed through the segmenting principle. The greenhouse effect, which is a multi-step atmospheric process involving solar radiation, energy conversion, atmospheric chemistry, and thermal dynamics, is broken into 12 discrete panels that each cover one part of the mechanism. This chunking approach allows readers to fully process each stage before moving to the next, rather than being overwhelmed by the full complexity presented all at once. The pretraining principle is built into the comic's structure: the earlier panels (2–4) introduce foundational concepts—sunlight arriving, reflection, infrared radiation—individually before the reader encounters the more complex idea of greenhouse gases trapping heat (Panels 5–6). By Panel 9, when molecular structures and chemical formulas appear (CO2, H2O, CH4), the reader already understands what these gases do functionally, so the chemical details add depth rather than confusion. This progressive scaffolding ensures that each new concept builds on established understanding.

Storytelling and Narrative Engagement

Storytelling principles are central to the comic's design, even within a simple, diagram-driven format. Research shows that narrative structure enhances information retention compared to bare factual presentation (Glonek & King, 2014), and that sensory and emotional engagement during story processing activate multiple brain regions, strengthening memory encoding (Chow et al., 2014; González et al., 2006). The consistent narrator character provides a storytelling thread that transforms what could be a dry scientific explanation into a guided experience. The character asks questions ("What are greenhouse gases?" in Panel 7) that mirror what the reader might be wondering, creating a sense of shared inquiry and dialogue. This technique aligns with Chibana's (2015) TED storytelling technique of immersing your audience and bringing characters to life. The comic also follows a clear narrative arc: introduction (Panel 1), building understanding (Panels 2–9), complication (Panels 10–11, where warming becomes a problem), and resolution with a call to action (Panel 12). This structure incorporates several of Humm's (2025) five core story components: location (Earth and its atmosphere), action (heat being trapped), thoughts (the narrator's curiosity), and dialogue (the narrator's questions and explanations). As Tolisano emphasizes, "digital storytelling is not about creating media—it's about creating meaning."

Personalization Principle

The personalization principle (Mayer) is applied through the narrator's conversational, informal tone. Rather than clinical scientific language ("Solar radiation undergoes partial albedo reflection..."), the comic uses approachable phrases like "Let's learn about the greenhouse effect!" and "This warms the planet!" Research shows that conversational language using first and second person enhances learning outcomes compared to formal academic register, because it creates a sense of social partnership between the learner and the material (Mayer). The narrator character also serves as a relatable guide—a fellow learner rather than an authority figure—which reduces the perceived social distance between the reader and the scientific content. This is particularly important given the target audience of university students who may feel intimidated by atmospheric science.

Active Learning: ICAP Framework and Merrill's Principles

Active learning principles are supported through the comic's design. While a comic is primarily a consumption-based medium, it encourages engagement beyond passive receiving. Within the ICAP Framework (Chi & Wylie, 2014), the comic moves readers from passive reception toward active engagement by prompting them to follow a sequential process, connect each step to the next, and build a cumulative mental model of the greenhouse effect. The question in Panel 7 ("What are greenhouse gases?") creates a moment of constructive engagement where readers naturally pause to consider what they already know before the answer is provided in Panels 8–9. The climate change framing satisfies Merrill's principle of authentic problems (2002): the greenhouse effect is not an abstract classroom exercise but a real-world issue that affects every reader's life, making the learning immediately meaningful. The early panels serve as scaffolding, building foundational understanding step by step before the more complex interactions are introduced, which reflects the active learning research finding that "complexity does not always equal better outcomes" and that the simplest approaches often work best. The final panel's call to action ("So, we need to protect our planet!") supports Merrill's integration principle by connecting new scientific knowledge to the reader's real-world behavior and decision-making, ensuring the learning extends beyond the comic itself.

Universal Design for Learning (UDL) and Accessibility

Universal Design for Learning (UDL) considerations are embedded throughout the comic's design, aligned with the three UDL networks. For Recognition Networks (the "what" of learning), the comic provides multiple means of representation by presenting information through both visual diagrams and text simultaneously, supporting learners who are stronger in either the visual or verbal channel. Key concepts are highlighted through labeled diagrams, and molecular formulas (CO2, H2O, CH4) provide precise scientific notation alongside plain-language explanations. For Strategic Networks (the "how"), the sequential panel structure provides a clear, logical flow that supports executive function by guiding the reader through the process one step at a time. For Affective Networks (the "why"), the friendly narrator character and relatable topic create emotional engagement and reduce anxiety around scientific content.

The black-and-white manga art style naturally provides high contrast between elements, meeting WCAG accessibility guidelines without additional modification. The clean visual style with labeled diagrams ensures that the science is accessible even to readers with no prior knowledge of atmospheric science. Plain language is used throughout, avoiding unnecessary jargon. Alt-text descriptions are provided for the comic image to support screen reader users. These design choices follow the inclusive design principle of "solve for one, extend to many" (Holmes, 2020): designing for accessibility benefits all learners, not just those with specific needs. As Gernsbacher (2015) demonstrated with video captions, accessibility features originally designed for specific populations often enhance the learning experience for everyone.
