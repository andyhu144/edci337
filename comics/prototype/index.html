The Greenhouse Effect

A 12-panel manga-style educational comic explaining how greenhouse gases trap heat in the atmosphere and contribute to climate change.

Andy HuFebruary 2026Comics Prototype · 15%
Figure 1: "The Greenhouse Effect" prototype (12 panels, manga-style, black and white)
Phase 1: Understand

Challenge & Audience

Climate change is widely discussed but poorly understood at the mechanistic level. University students hear terms like "greenhouse gases" and "carbon emissions" constantly, but most cannot walk through how the greenhouse effect actually works. This gap between awareness and understanding limits meaningful engagement with environmental issues.

The audience is university students (ages 18-25) in introductory science or general education courses. They are time-strapped, environmentally concerned but scientifically uncertain, and likely to share well-designed educational content with peers. A typical student knows carbon is bad and the planet is warming, but cannot explain the steps between sunlight entering the atmosphere and the planet heating up.

People with limited knowledge of atmospheric science need a clear, visual, step-by-step explanation of how the greenhouse effect works, so that they can understand the mechanism behind climate change and engage in informed environmental discussions.

Learning Objectives

  • Describe the greenhouse effect step by step, from solar radiation entry to heat trapping
  • Explain the difference between incoming solar radiation and outgoing infrared radiation
  • Identify the major greenhouse gases (CO2, CH4, H2O) and their role in trapping heat
  • Explain why the greenhouse effect becomes a problem when amplified by human activity
  • Connect the greenhouse effect to climate change and articulate why reducing emissions matters
Phase 2: Plan

Storyboard & Panel Script

I chose a narrator-guided walkthrough with two consistent manga characters leading the reader through the process step by step. This naturally applies the personalization principle (conversational tone), the segmenting principle (one concept per panel), and the contiguity principle (labels placed directly on diagrams). Each panel focuses on one step of the greenhouse effect, following the sequential logic of the atmospheric process itself.

Panel 1
Introduction
Girl and boy characters, simple background.
"Let's learn about the greenhouse effect!"
Panel 2
Sunlight Arrives
View from space, sun rays streaming toward Earth.
"The Sun shines down on Earth."
Panel 3
Reflection
Arrows bouncing off Earth's surface back into space.
"Some sunlight is reflected back into space."
Panel 4
Infrared Radiation
Wavy arrows radiating upward from Earth's surface.
"The Earth emits heat as infrared radiation."
Panel 5
Gases Trap Heat
Atmospheric band deflecting arrows back downward.
"Greenhouse gases trap some of this heat."
Panel 6
Heat Trapped
Arrows bouncing between surface and gas layer.
Label: "Heat Trapped"
Panel 7
Question
Girl with curious expression.
"What are greenhouse gases?"
Panel 8
Naming the Gases
Earth's surface with heat waves rising.
"Carbon dioxide, methane, and water vapor."
Panel 9
Molecular Diagrams
Molecular structure diagrams.
Labels: CO2, H2O, CH4
Panel 10
Heat Stays In
Arrows looping between surface and atmosphere.
"These gases keep heat in the atmosphere."
Panel 11
Planet Warming
Earth from space with heat glow radiating outward.
"This warms the planet!"
Panel 12
Call to Action
Both characters facing the viewer, melting icecaps behind.
"Too much warming leads to climate change. We need to protect our planet!"
Theory Applied

How Course Principles Shaped the Design

Dual Coding & Multimedia Principle Each panel combines visual diagrams with brief text, encoding the same concept through both visual and verbal channels. Panel 4 shows wavy lines (visual) while the caption explains infrared radiation (verbal). This builds stronger mental representations (Schnotz, 2022).
Cognitive Load: Extraneous Clean black-and-white manga style with no decorative elements (coherence). Labels placed directly on diagrams (contiguity). Bold key terms like "Infrared Radiation" and "CO2" direct attention (signaling). Every element serves the learning objective.
Cognitive Load: Intrinsic The multi-step atmospheric process is broken into 12 discrete panels, each covering one concept (segmenting). Earlier panels introduce foundational ideas before complex chemistry appears in Panels 8-9 (pretraining).
Storytelling & Personalization Consistent narrator characters provide narrative continuity. Conversational tone ("Let's learn about...") applies the personalization principle. Panel 7's question ("What are greenhouse gases?") creates a moment of shared inquiry (Glonek & King, 2014).
Active Learning (ICAP & Merrill) The comic moves readers from passive toward active engagement by building a cumulative mental model. Panel 7's question creates constructive engagement. The climate change framing satisfies Merrill's authentic problems principle. Panel 12's call to action fulfils the integration principle.
UDL & Accessibility Multiple representations (visual diagrams + text) support recognition networks. Sequential structure guides strategic networks. Friendly characters support affective networks. High-contrast black-and-white art meets WCAG guidelines. Alt text provided for screen readers.
Generative AI Acknowledgement: Midjourney was used to generate the comic panel illustrations. Adobe Photoshop was used for editing and refinements. All text, educational content, design documentation, and theoretical analysis are original work.
