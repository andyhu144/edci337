EDCI 337 — Multimedia Challenge: Comics

The Greenhouse Effect

Understanding Climate Change, One Panel at a Time
Andy Hu  |  February 21, 2026  |  University of Victoria

I'm creating an educational comic about the greenhouse effect and how it contributes to climate change. I chose this topic because climate change is one of the most important issues facing our generation, yet many university students struggle to explain the basic science behind it. Terms like "greenhouse gases," "infrared radiation," and "carbon emissions" come up constantly in the news and in campus conversations, but I've noticed that most people, myself included, couldn't clearly walk through the actual mechanism of how the greenhouse effect works.

My goal is to break down this invisible atmospheric process into a simple, visual, step-by-step explanation that anyone can understand in a few minutes. The comic format works perfectly here because the greenhouse effect is inherently sequential: sunlight comes in, gets absorbed, heat gets emitted, gases trap it, the planet warms. Each step maps naturally onto a panel, making the science feel like a story rather than a lecture.

The Process

Phase 1: Understand (Discover, Interpret, Specify)

Describe the Challenge

In the case of climate change, which is widely discussed but poorly understood at the mechanistic level, university students are very familiar with the terms "greenhouse effect" and "carbon emissions" in the news, on social media and in campus discussions, but they are unable to explain how greenhouse gases actually absorb heat from the atmosphere. This gap between awareness and understanding limits the ability to meaningfully engage in environmental discussions, to evaluate proposed solutions, and to make informed decisions about sustainable living in their everyday lives.

Context and Audience

The primary audience for this comic is university students, aged between 18 and 25, who are taking introductory geography, geography or general education courses or simply want to understand the science of climate change. Many of them hear about climate change daily on the news and on social media but are unable to explain the basic mechanism of it when they are asked. This creates a gap: they are interested in the subject but lack the scientific vocabulary and conceptual understanding to discuss it with confidence.

They are also the most time-strapped in terms of education, juggling several courses, jobs and social life, so learning materials that are engaging and can be consumed in a short time are ideal. They are concerned about the environment, but are limited to the surface level, knowing that carbon is bad and that the planet is warming, rather than a real scientific literacy of the reasons why this happens. Besides, they are likely to share well-designed educational content with their peers on social media or in group chats, which means that a successful graphic could reach many more than its original reader.

A typical example is a second-year university student who has heard of the greenhouse effect several times and knows something about carbon dioxide and warming but cannot explain the steps involved in the mechanism of how sunlight enters the atmosphere, gets converted to heat, and is trapped by greenhouse gases. Such a student would profit from a graphical explanation, which would link the steps of the process, each before the next. He might also confuse the greenhouse effect with the ozone layer, and believe that the greenhouse effect is an inherently bad thing rather than the result of a natural process that has been intensified by human activity.

The comic has to be accessible, but not condescending. For example, an extreme case might be a student who is deeply anxious about climate change, but who is not sufficiently familiar with the subject to participate in discussions and to critically judge proposed solutions. Without mechanistic knowledge, they will accept or reject climate change on emotional or social grounds, rather than based on scientific arguments. On the other hand, a student who is skeptical about climate change might profit from seeing the simple physics of how greenhouse gases trap heat, without political framing, just science. Contrasting backgrounds and foregrounds, legible fonts, logical and sequential structure, a clear, enticing style and descriptive alt-text for screen-readers are all important for accessibility.

POV Statement

Those who have limited knowledge of atmospheric science need a clear, visual, step-by-step explanation of how the greenhouse effect works, so that they can understand the mechanism behind climate change, can engage in meaningful discussions about the environment and can make informed decisions about sustainable development.

Learning Objectives

Main Learning Objective

After reading this comic, the student will be able to describe the greenhouse effect in steps, from the entry of the solar radiation to the trapping of heat by the greenhouse gases as well as explain in general how it affects the climate.

Sub-Learning Objectives

  • Students will be able to explain the difference between the incoming solar radiation and the outgoing infrared radiation in the context of the Earth's energy balance.
  • Students will be able to identify the major greenhouse gases (carbon dioxide, methane and water vapor) and describe their role in trapping heat in the atmosphere.
  • Students will be able to explain why the greenhouse effect, although a natural and essential process for life on earth, becomes a problem when amplified by increased greenhouse gas concentrations from human activity.
  • Students will be able to connect the greenhouse effect with climate change and articulate why reducing greenhouse gas emissions is important for the future of the planet.

Secret Learning Objectives

  • Science education should create a sense of scientific agency, so that students feel capable of participating in the discussion on the environment, rather than feeling excluded from it.
  • By showing how the viewer can understand complex atmospheric processes through the medium of accessible visual means, he develops scientific literacy and shows that science is not reserved for experts.
  • Reflect metacognitively on how step-by-step visual explanations of abstract processes make them easier to learn and remember, and foster an appreciation of multimedia as a learning tool.

Phase 2: Plan (Ideate, Sketch, Elaborate)

Ideation

Using the learning objectives for this lesson, I created a number of approaches for teaching about the greenhouse effect through a comic strip. I evaluated each approach against the multimedia design principles we learned about in EDCI 337:

Idea 1: Infographic-style panels. Each panel would show a labeled diagram of a step in the greenhouse effect, similar to a figure in a textbook, but in the format of a comic. This would be informative but also perhaps dry and passive, like a textbook with illustrations than a true comic. The latest research on storytelling and memory suggests that the storytelling activates multiple brain regions and enhances the recall of facts (Glonek & King, 2014). This approach would also miss the opportunity to leverage the personalization principle (Mayer), because the language of informational labels is usually formal and dry.

Idea 2: Anthropomorphized gas molecules as characters. CO2, methane, and water vapor molecules would be characters with distinct personalities, and the story would follow them as they trap heat. This approach has strong storytelling potential and would create emotional engagement through character development. However, it risks overcomplicating the science by attributing motivations and actions to molecules, potentially creating misconceptions. It could also increase extraneous cognitive load (Sweller) by forcing readers to track multiple characters, their personalities, and their relationships while simultaneously learning the atmospheric process. The split attention between character narrative and scientific content could undermine learning rather than support it.

Idea 3: A narrator-guided walkthrough with a consistent character. Two manga-like characters lead the reader through the greenhouse effect step by step, with each panel showing one step of the process in a clear graphic, with simple captions. The characters ask the reader questions, e.g. "What are greenhouse gases?" and answer them, creating a conversational, approachable tone. The characters provide narrative continuity and emotional engagement without overcomplicating the science. The approach naturally embodies the personalization principle (conversational tone), the segmenting principle (one step per panel), and the contiguity principle (labels placed directly on diagrams).

Idea 4: A time-travel narrative. A student travels back in time to see Earth without greenhouse gases (frozen), then to the present (comfortable), then to the future (overheated). While dramatic and emotionally compelling, this approach splits attention between the time-travel plot and the actual science, increasing extraneous cognitive load. It also requires too many panels for narrative setup (establishing the time-travel concept, introducing the character's motivation), leaving fewer panels for the actual scientific explanation. Given the 12-panel constraint, this approach would sacrifice depth of explanation for narrative complexity.

I finally chose Idea 3 as my final direction because it was the best to meet the learning goals and keep the comic simple, clear and interesting. The narrative thread maintains reader interest (Glonek & King, 2014) without introducing unnecessary complexity, the step-by-step structure of the panels fits the sequential nature of the greenhouse effect, and the conversational tone supports the personalization principle (Mayer). Each panel focuses on a single concept, which supports the management of mental load through segmentation (Sweller). Tribull (2017) argues that sequential visual narrative is particularly effective in communicating scientific processes, as it follows the step-by-step logic of scientific thinking, and Morrison, Bryan, and Chilcoat (2002) found that the process of making a comic engages students by giving them ownership of the visual representation of concepts and increases comprehension.

Storyboard / Script

Below is the panel-by-panel script that guided the comic's creation.

Panel 1 — Introduction
Scene: A girl shown from the waist up, turned to the side with a listening expression. A boy standing forward facing, pointing upwards with his right hand. Simple background.
Text: "Let's learn about the greenhouse effect!"
Panel 2 — Sunlight Arrives
Scene: View from space looking down at Earth. The sun in the upper right with rays streaming down toward the planet's curved surface. Stars and dark space above, thin atmosphere visible along the horizon.
Text: "The Sun shines down on Earth."
Panel 3 — Reflection and Absorption
Scene: Earth's surface and atmosphere from space. Arrows pointing down as incoming sunlight, some arrows bouncing back upward off the surface into space showing reflection. Cloud layers along the curved horizon, stars above.
Text: "Some of the sunlight is reflected back into space." Label: "Absorption"
Panel 4 — Infrared Radiation
Scene: Earth's curved surface across the middle of the frame. Wavy zigzag arrows radiating upward from the surface into space, representing heat energy.
Text: "The Earth then emits heat as infrared radiation." Label: "Infrared Radiation"
Panel 5 — Greenhouse Gases Trap Heat
Scene: Earth centered in the bottom of the frame. Surface at the bottom with wavy infrared arrows rising upward. A hazy band in the atmosphere representing greenhouse gases deflects some arrows back downward while a few pass through into space.
Text: "Greenhouse gases in the atmosphere trap some of this heat."
Panel 6 — Heat Trapped (Visual)
Scene: Atmospheric diagram showing Earth's surface at the bottom and a cloudy gas layer above. Wavy heat arrows bouncing back and forth between the ground and the gas layer, unable to escape.
Label: "Heat Trapped"
Panel 7 — Question Panel
Scene: A girl shown from the shoulders up with a curious expression, head tilted slightly, looking to the left.
Text: "What are greenhouse gases?"
Panel 8 — Naming the Gases
Scene: Earth's curved surface along the bottom with wavy lines rising upward representing heat. Cloudy atmospheric sky background.
Text: "They include carbon dioxide, methane, and water vapor."
Panel 9 — Molecular Diagrams
Scene: (to be added)
Text: "CO2" / "H2O" / "CH4" (labels on diagrams)
Panel 10 — Heat Stays In
Scene: Atmospheric cross-section with Earth's surface along the bottom and a cloudy gas layer above. Wavy arrows rise from the surface and curve back downward off the gas layer, looping between the ground and atmosphere.
Text: "These gases keep heat in the atmosphere." Label: "Heat Stays In"
Panel 11 — Planet Warming
Scene: Full view of Earth from space, centered. Wavy heat lines and a glow radiating outward from the planet's surface, representing the planet heating up.
Text: "This warms the planet!" Label: "Warming Up!"
Panel 12 — Consequences and Call to Action
Scene: Wider panel. Two characters standing side by side facing the viewer with excited expressions, the girl and the boy. A duplicate of the pair is visible beside them. Behind them a landscape of melting icecaps and rising water.
Text: "Too much warming leads to climate change... So, we need to protect our planet!"

Theory Applied

This comic was guided by several theories of multimedia learning covered in EDCI 337. Below I explain each theory, how it applies to the comic's design, and why it supports the learning objectives.

Mayer's Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning & Dual Coding

The comic employs dual coding theory (Paivio) by combining visual illustrations with brief text in each panel, allowing readers to process the greenhouse effect through both the visual and verbal channels at the same time. For example, Panel 4 shows wavy lines radiating upward from the Earth (visual channel) while the caption explains "The Earth then emits heat as infrared radiation" (verbal channel). Because the same concept is encoded through two separate neural pathways, the reader builds a stronger, more retrievable mental representation of the process (Schnotz, 2022). This is the core of Mayer's multimedia principle: people learn more effectively from words and pictures together than from words alone. Each step of the greenhouse effect is both shown and described, ensuring that readers who are stronger in either channel can still access the content.

Cognitive Load Theory: Managing Extraneous Load

Cognitive load theory (Sweller) is the basis of several design decisions that aim to reduce extraneous cognitive load, the unnecessary mental effort spent on poor design rather than on actual learning. The coherence principle is applied through the clean black-and-white manga art style without decorative elements; every visual element serves the learning objective. The contiguity principle is applied by placing text labels directly on or adjacent to the relevant visual element. For instance, the label "Infrared Radiation" appears right next to the heat waves in Panel 4, and "Heat Trapped" is placed directly over the trapping diagram in Panel 6. This spatial proximity reduces the mental effort required for readers to make connections between text and images. The signaling principle is used through bold labels like "Absorption," "Infrared Radiation," "CO2," "H2O," "CH4," and "Heat Stays In" that direct the reader's attention to the most important scientific concepts in each panel. These labels function as visual cues, making the most important information stand out so that readers do not need to waste mental effort determining what is important.

Cognitive Load Theory: Managing Intrinsic Load

Intrinsic cognitive load is managed through the segmenting principle. The greenhouse effect, a multi-step process involving solar radiation, energy conversion, atmospheric chemistry, and thermal dynamics, is broken into 12 discrete panels that each cover one part of the mechanism, so that readers can process each step separately and fully rather than being overwhelmed by the full complexity at once. The pretraining principle is embedded in the comic's structure: the earlier panels (2–4) introduce foundational concepts (sunlight arriving, reflection, infrared radiation) separately before the more complex idea of greenhouse gases trapping heat (Panels 5–6). By Panel 9, when molecular structures and chemical formulas appear (CO2, H2O, CH4), the reader already understands the basic functioning of these gases, so the chemical details add depth rather than confusion. Each new concept is introduced on the basis of concepts already established.

Storytelling and Narrative Engagement

Storytelling principles are central to the design of the comic, even in its diagram-driven format. Research shows that narrative structures enhance information retention compared to bare factual presentation (Glonek & King, 2014), and that emotional and sensory engagement in story processing activates multiple brain regions, strengthening memory encoding (Chow et al., 2014; González et al., 2006). The consistent narrator character provides a narrative thread that transforms what could be a dry explanation into a guided experience. The character asks questions ("What are greenhouse gases?" in Panel 7) that echo what the reader might be wondering, creating a sense of shared inquiry and dialogue. This aligns with Chibana's (2015) TED storytelling technique of immersing your audience and bringing characters to life. The comic also follows a clear narrative arc: introduction (Panel 1), building understanding (Panels 2–9), complication (Panels 10–11, where warming becomes a problem), and resolution with a call to action (Panel 12). This structure involves several of Humm's (2025) five core story components: location (Earth and its atmosphere), action (heat being trapped), thoughts (the narrator's curiosity), and dialogue (the narrator's questions and explanations). As Tolisano emphasizes, "digital storytelling is not about creating media, it's about creating meaning."

Personalization Principle

The personalization principle (Mayer) is applied through the narrator's conversational, informal tone. Instead of clinical scientific language ("Solar radiation undergoes partial albedo reflection..."), the comic uses approachable phrases like "Let's learn about the greenhouse effect!" and "This warms the planet!" The narrator serves as a relatable guide rather than an authority figure, which reduces the social distance between the reader and the scientific content. This is especially important given the target audience of university students who may feel intimidated by atmospheric science.

Active Learning: ICAP Framework and Merrill's Principles

While a comic is primarily a consumption-based medium, active learning principles are also supported through its design. Within the ICAP Framework (Chi & Wylie, 2014), the comic moves readers from passive reception toward active engagement by encouraging them to follow a sequential process, link each step to the next, and build a cumulative mental model of the greenhouse effect. The question in Panel 7 ("What are greenhouse gases?") creates a moment of constructive engagement where readers naturally pause to consider what they already know before the answer is provided in Panels 8–9. The climate change framing satisfies Merrill's principle of authentic problems (2002): the greenhouse effect is not an abstract classroom exercise but a real-world issue that affects every reader's life, making the learning immediately meaningful. The early panels serve as scaffolding, building foundational understanding before more complex interactions are introduced, which follows the active learning research finding that "complexity does not always equal better outcomes" and that the simplest approaches often work best. The final panel's call to action ("So, we need to protect our planet!") fulfils Merrill's integration principle by linking new scientific knowledge to real-world behavior and decision-making, extending the learning beyond the comic itself.

Universal Design for Learning (UDL) and Accessibility

Universal Design for Learning (UDL) considerations are embedded throughout the comic's design, aligned with the three UDL networks. For Recognition Networks (the "what" of learning), the comic provides multiple representations of information by simultaneously presenting visual diagrams and text, supporting learners who are stronger in either the visual or verbal channel. Key concepts are highlighted through labeled diagrams, and molecular formulas (CO2, H2O, CH4) provide precise scientific notation alongside plain-language explanations. For Strategic Networks (the "how"), the sequential panel structure and logical progression guide the reader through the process one step at a time. For Affective Networks (the "why"), the friendly narrator character and relatable topic reduce anxiety around scientific content.

The black-and-white manga art style naturally provides high contrast between elements, conforming to WCAG accessibility guidelines without additional effort. Plain language is used throughout, avoiding unnecessary jargon, and alt-text descriptions are provided for the comic image to support screen reader users. These design choices follow the inclusive design principle of "solve for one, extend to many" (Holmes, 2020): designing for accessibility benefits all learners, not just those with specific needs. As Gernsbacher (2015) demonstrated with video captions, accessibility features originally designed for specific populations often enhance the learning experience for everyone.
