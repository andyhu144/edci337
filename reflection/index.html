Revisiting the Plan

Where I Started vs Where I Ended

In my Flight Path, I wrote that I wanted to understand the "why" behind design decisions I was already making instinctively. I wanted cognitive load theory, dual coding, and contiguity to become tools I could name and use deliberately. Looking back, that goal was met more completely than I expected. By the time I wrote the Multimedia Critique, I was evaluating real educational resources against a rubric I built from these principles. By the time I built the OER, I was creating interactive demonstrations that let other people experience those principles firsthand. That progression, from learning vocabulary to building tools that teach the concepts to someone else, is the clearest evidence that the learning stuck.

I also set a goal around formalizing my design process. The comics prototype was the first time I went through the full understand, plan, prototype, feedback, refine cycle. In my normal workflow, I would have started building immediately and revised later. The structure forced me to think before I built, and the result was stronger for it. The peer feedback process reinforced that, because reviewers caught issues I had been too close to the work to see.

The course objectives I feel strongest about are applying multimedia design principles and engaging in design thinking. The comics project required narrative structure to carry educational content, which addressed the storytelling objective. The OER project required me to build an interactive website that teaches design principles using the same principles it teaches, which is about as direct an application of course theory as I can imagine. The accessibility objective shifted my thinking from compliance to design philosophy, and that shift shows up in every project I built for this course.

The Turbulence

What Was Hardest

The most challenging part was what I predicted it would be: slowing down to reflect. In my professional work, the feedback loop is fast. Build, deploy, measure, adjust. The course required a different kind of processing. Reading about cognitive load theory, sitting with it, connecting it to my own experience, and then writing about that connection in a way that was honest rather than performative took more effort than I expected. The theory itself was not difficult. The reflection was.

Scope discipline was the other challenge. The comics prototype took significantly more time than it needed to because I kept refining the visual quality rather than focusing on the educational design. The OER project had the same pull, and I had to consciously stop adding interactive features and ask whether each one actually served a learning objective or just looked impressive. That tension between craft and purpose is something the coherence principle addresses directly, and it was useful to have the vocabulary for it even when I was struggling to apply it to my own work.

The peer feedback process was also harder than expected, not because writing feedback is difficult, but because receiving it requires a kind of openness that does not come naturally when you are used to being the one making the final call. Having someone point out that my comic's text density violated the segmenting principle was humbling and useful in equal measure. It forced me to practice what I was preaching about cognitive load rather than just writing about it.

Evidence of Growth

What Changed in My Thinking

The clearest evidence of growth is in how I evaluate interfaces now. Before the course, I would look at a design and assess it based on aesthetics and usability feel. Now I run through the principles automatically. Is the layout maintaining contiguity? Is the content segmented appropriately? Is there redundancy across channels? Are there multiple means of representation for diverse learners? These are not things I have to consciously remember to check. They have become part of how I see design.

The substantive posts track this shift. The first post (Theories) reads like someone discovering new vocabulary for familiar experiences. The Accessibility post reads like someone who has started integrating that vocabulary into their default way of thinking. The Multimedia Critique required building an assessment tool from scratch and applying it independently. And the OER required teaching the principles to someone else through interactive demonstrations, which is the highest level of understanding because you cannot teach something you have not internalized.

The accessibility unit produced the most lasting change. Kat Holmes' reframe, that disability is a mismatch between a person's abilities and their environment, shifted my understanding from accommodation to design responsibility. I built the OER's contrast checker because I wanted to make the abstract concept of WCAG compliance into something tangible that anyone could use in ten seconds. That tool exists because of what I learned in week 4 of this course, and I have already used it in my own product work to catch contrast issues I would have previously missed.

The OER project as a whole is probably the strongest evidence. Building an interactive website that uses cognitive load principles to teach cognitive load principles, that uses the contiguity principle in its own layout while teaching the contiguity principle through a drag-and-drop demo, and that meets WCAG accessibility standards while teaching accessibility standards, required a level of integration across all the course content that no individual assignment could have produced. The medium is the message, and the OER is the proof that I understood the material well enough to apply it recursively.

Next Destination

Where These Skills Go From Here

The potential for multimedia in education is significant, but the impact depends on how deliberately it is designed. The tools are not the constraint. The constraint is whether the people building educational content understand the cognitive science behind how learning works. This course gave me that understanding in a way that is directly applicable to the products I build and the team I lead.

1
Accessibility-First Product Design Integrate WCAG audits and UDL principles into my company's design system from the component level up. The contrast checker from the OER is already part of my workflow. I want to build that kind of tooling into every design review.
2
Narrated Product Documentation Apply the modality principle by creating video walkthroughs with narration for complex features instead of defaulting to text-only documentation. The research is clear on this one, and I no longer have the excuse of not knowing the theory.
3
Constructive Onboarding Redesign Redesign onboarding flows using Merrill's principles and the ICAP framework. Move users from passive tooltip tours to constructive, task-based learning where they build something real with guidance. The OER project proved this approach works.

Each of these goals connects directly to something I learned in this course and something I need to do better in my work. The theories are not abstract. They are actionable. That is the most valuable thing I am taking from EDCI 337: a set of principles that I can use immediately, explain clearly to my team, and apply to real design decisions every day. The course did not teach me how to design. It taught me how to think about design, which is more durable and more transferable than any specific technique.
