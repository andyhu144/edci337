Flight Path

Where I'm coming from, where I want this course to take me, and what I expect to be hard.

Andy Hu January 2026
Current Coordinates

I build software for a living.

I run a small software company — design, development, deployment, the full cycle. Most of my work sits at the intersection of frontend engineering and product design. I've shipped applications that real people use daily, built interfaces that had to communicate complex information clearly, and learned through a lot of trial and error what makes something click for a user and what makes them bounce.

My instincts around multimedia are strong but informal. I know when a layout feels cluttered or when an animation distracts more than it guides. I can look at a dashboard and tell you in seconds whether it respects the user's attention or wastes it. But if you asked me to name the principle behind that judgment, I'd struggle. I'd say something like "it just feels off" — which is fine when you're iterating on your own product, but not exactly a framework you can teach someone else or defend in a meeting.

That gap is why I'm here.

I've spent years learning what works. I want to understand why it works — and more importantly, why certain things don't work even when they look like they should.

On the storytelling side, I've done some of this naturally through product content — onboarding flows, feature announcements, the kind of micro-copy that turns a confusing feature into something intuitive. But I've never formally studied narrative structure or how storytelling principles map onto educational design. That's new territory for me.

Mapping the Route

What I want to walk away with.

I'm not here to check a box. There are specific things I want to be able to do by the end of this course that I genuinely can't do right now:

Ground my design decisions in cognitive theory Cognitive load, dual coding, contiguity — I want the vocabulary to explain why a layout works, not just feel it.
Formalize the design process I'm used to building fast and iterating. I want to learn what a structured design thinking approach adds — empathize, define, ideate, prototype, test — and where it makes my current process better.
Use storytelling with intention Not just "make it engaging" — I want to understand why narrative works neurologically and how to structure educational content around it.
Build something accessible from the start Accessibility has always been something I've patched in later. I want to design with UDL principles baked in from day one — not as an afterthought.

The course objectives around multimedia design principles and interactive design align directly with problems I solve every week. The difference is I want to solve them better — with the theoretical foundation that turns good instincts into repeatable methodology.

Anticipating Turbulence

Where I expect to struggle.

Honestly? The academic framing. I think in prototypes, user feedback, and deployment cycles. My natural language is "ship it, test it, learn from it." Sitting with theory — reading about principles, reflecting on them in writing, connecting them to broader educational contexts — that's a different rhythm. It's slower. It's more deliberate. And it's not something I get to practice in my day-to-day work where speed is a competitive advantage.

The other thing is scope discipline. When I see a project brief, my instinct is to over-engineer it. I'll spend three days building something that was supposed to take three hours because I got pulled into making it perfect. For this course, I'll need to balance quality with time — especially juggling coursework alongside running a business. The goal isn't to build the most impressive artifact every time. It's to learn the most from each one.

My strategy is straightforward: treat each assignment like a client project with a real deadline. Define the scope, do the work, ship it, reflect on it. The reflection part is the muscle I need to build — I'm used to looking forward to the next thing, not sitting with what I just made and analyzing why it did or didn't work.

The biggest shift for me won't be learning the tools or building the projects — it'll be learning to slow down enough to understand the theory behind what I already do instinctively.
