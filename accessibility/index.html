Reframing the Problem

Disability Is a Design Failure

In software, I have always thought of accessibility as a compliance checklist. Add alt text, check contrast ratios, make sure the tab order is logical, run an audit, fix the flagged items. It is not that I considered it unimportant - it is that I treated it as a layer to apply at the end rather than a lens to design through from the beginning.

Kat Holmes reframes this completely: "Disability is a mismatch between a person's abilities and their environment" (Holmes, 2020). That shifts the responsibility from the user to the designer. If someone cannot use what I built, that is not their limitation. It is mine. I built an environment that excludes them. That framing changes how I think about every design decision.

The practical implication is that accessibility features almost always benefit everyone, not just the people they were originally designed for. Captions help hearing-impaired users, but they also help someone watching a video in a noisy room, someone learning in a second language, and someone who processes written information faster than spoken. Gernsbacher (2015) found that captions improve comprehension, attention, and memory across all viewers. That is the argument that should end any debate about whether captions are "worth the effort."

Holmes (2020)

Three Inclusive Design Principles

1
Recognize Exclusion Identify who your design choices leave out. Every design decision includes some people and excludes others.
2
Solve for One, Extend to Many A solution designed for someone with a permanent disability often benefits people with temporary or situational limitations.
3
Learn from Diversity Include people who have already adapted to non-inclusive environments. They have insights that typical usability testing misses.

The second principle - solve for one, extend to many - is the one that resonates most with my work. When I build a high-contrast mode for a visually impaired user, it also helps someone using their laptop in direct sunlight. When I add keyboard navigation for a user who cannot use a mouse, it also helps a power user who finds keyboard shortcuts faster. The edge case is rarely the edge. It is a wider need that the mainstream just has not articulated yet.

CAST Framework

Universal Design for Learning

UDL takes inclusive design and applies it specifically to learning. Instead of designing for an "average" learner and then retrofitting accommodations, UDL builds flexibility into the design from the start. The framework organizes around three brain networks - why we learn, what we learn, and how we show what we know.

Affective Networks
Engagement
  • Multiple ways to sustain motivation
  • User control over speed and sequence
  • Authentic, relevant challenges
  • Self-assessment opportunities
Recognition Networks
Representation
  • Alt text, captions, transcripts
  • Multiple formats for content
  • Key terms highlighted
  • Background knowledge activated
Strategic Networks
Action & Expression
  • Multiple ways to demonstrate knowledge
  • Scaffolded practice
  • Tool fluency support
  • Executive function aids
In My Work

What I Am Changing

The most immediate change for me is moving accessibility from a QA step to a design step. In practice, that means checking contrast ratios while choosing colors, not after the design is built. It means writing alt text when placing images, not during a pre-launch sweep. It means testing keyboard navigation as I build each component, not running an automated audit at the end and patching the failures.

The UDL framework also connects directly to last week's active learning models. Providing multiple means of representation is essentially applying the modality principle at scale. Offering multiple means of action and expression creates constructive and interactive engagement opportunities rather than limiting learners to a single passive format. The theories are not isolated. They reinforce each other.

Something I had not considered before is the accessibility of text itself. Sans-serif fonts, plain language, proper heading hierarchy, descriptive link text instead of "click here" - these are not just style preferences. They are accessibility requirements that affect how screen readers parse a page and how quickly any user can scan for relevant information. This overlaps with the signaling principle from Mayer: clear structure reduces cognitive load for everyone.

One specific thing I plan to implement in my next project is providing transcripts as a default alongside all video content. The ROI argument is convincing on its own - captions increase watch time and audience reach - but the real reason is that it is the right design choice. If someone cannot access the content in its primary format, I have not designed it well enough. That is the reframe from this week that I expect to carry into everything I build going forward.
